Does a Moving-Average Crossover Beat Buy-and-Hold?

An out-of-sample backtest of the 20/50 EMA crossover strategy on five FTSE 100 equities, 2022–2024

A quantitative finance study · Test period Jul 2022 – Dec 2024

Abstract

The exponential-moving-average (EMA) crossover is one of the most widely taught trend-following trading rules: go long when a fast moving average rises above a slow one, and exit when it falls back below. This study tests whether the canonical 20-day/50-day EMA crossover delivers superior risk-adjusted returns compared with a passive buy-and-hold benchmark, across five large-cap FTSE 100 stocks (Shell, AstraZeneca, HSBC, Tesco and BAE Systems). Daily dividend-adjusted prices from 2019–2024 are split into an in-sample warm-up period and a strictly out-of-sample test period (July 2022 onward). The strategy is simulated with realistic 0.1% per-trade transaction costs and a one-day execution lag to eliminate look-ahead bias. Performance is judged on the annualised Sharpe ratio, maximum drawdown and total return, and the result is stress-tested across a grid of EMA parameter pairs. The headline figures below summarise the out-of-sample outcome.

0 / 5 stocks where the EMA strategy beat buy-and-hold on Sharpe ratio
−0.03 average EMA Sharpe ratio (out-of-sample)
+0.44 average buy-and-hold Sharpe ratio
1 / 5 stocks where the EMA strategy had a shallower drawdown

Headline finding: over this out-of-sample window the 20/50 EMA crossover underperformed a passive buy-and-hold on every stock by risk-adjusted return.

1.Aim & research question

The central question is deliberately narrow and falsifiable:

Research question Over a genuinely out-of-sample period, does a 20/50 EMA crossover strategy produce a higher annualised Sharpe ratio — and a shallower maximum drawdown — than simply buying and holding the same stock, after accounting for trading costs?

Three sub-objectives follow from this:

  1. Fair measurement. Build a backtest that is free of look-ahead bias, charges transaction costs on every trade, and evaluates the strategy only on data it was never tuned on.
  2. Cross-sectional comparison. Apply the identical rule to five different sectors (energy, pharma, banking, retail, defence) to see whether any edge is stock-specific or general.
  3. Robustness. Re-run the strategy across a grid of fast/slow EMA windows to check whether the conclusion depends on the specific 20/50 choice.
