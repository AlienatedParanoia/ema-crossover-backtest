Section 6

Conclusion & limitations

6.1Findings

The research question was: over a genuinely out-of-sample period, does a 20/50 EMA crossover produce a higher Sharpe ratio and a shallower drawdown than buying and holding, after costs? The evidence answers clearly: no.

  • Risk-adjusted return: buy-and-hold wins 5–0. The benchmark posted a higher Sharpe ratio on every single stock. Averaged across the universe, the EMA strategy returned a Sharpe of about −0.03 versus +0.44 for buy-and-hold — the difference between roughly flat risk-adjusted performance and a respectable one.
  • Absolute return: buy-and-hold wins 5–0. The gap was often large because the strategy sat in cash through major rallies — HSBC returned +46.5% held but only +5.3% traded, and BAE +36.7% versus +1.4%. On AstraZeneca the rule actively destroyed value, turning a −3.2% hold into a −17.5% loss through repeated whipsaws.
  • Drawdown: a single consolation. The one place the strategy helped was Tesco's maximum drawdown (−14.1% vs −26.5%), where moving to cash genuinely limited the downside. On the other four stocks it drew down more than simply holding — trading added risk rather than removing it.
  • Hindsight doesn't rescue it. Even when the best parameter pair is chosen with full knowledge of the test set (Section 5), the strategy only edges past buy-and-hold's Sharpe on three of five stocks — and the honest, pre-committed 20/50 rule lost on all five. The edge isn't hiding in a different parameter choice.

The economic intuition is straightforward. A long/flat trend rule is, in effect, a bet that avoiding downtrends outweighs missing the early part of uptrends. Over this particular 2022–2024 window — which contained sharp, V-shaped recoveries — the strategy was repeatedly whipsawed out near local bottoms and bought back in only after much of the rebound had already happened. The transaction costs then compounded the damage. Buying and holding, which is always invested, simply captured more of the upside.

6.2Limitations

The result should be read with the following caveats, in roughly descending order of importance:

  1. Sensitivity "best params" are in-sample to the test set. The optimal EMA windows reported in Section 5 are chosen by Sharpe ratio measured on the test data itself. They describe robustness, not a tradeable edge — a live trader cannot know them in advance. Only the pre-committed 20/50 run is a true out-of-sample result.
  2. Small, survivorship-biased universe. Five large-cap stocks that are still in the index today is a narrow and favourable sample. Companies that were delisted or collapsed are excluded by construction, which flatters any long-biased strategy.
  3. Simplified cost & execution model. Costs are a flat 0.1% per trade with no slippage, no bid–ask modelling, and fills assumed at the close. The benchmark pays no entry cost at all. Real-world frictions would erode the strategy more than the benchmark, since the strategy trades repeatedly.
  4. Long/flat only. The rule never shorts; on a sell signal it sits in cash earning nothing (the risk-free rate is used only in the Sharpe calculation, not credited to idle capital). This understates the opportunity cost of being out of the market.
  5. Single regime. The 2.5-year test window covers one particular market environment. Trend-following rules are regime-dependent — they tend to shine in sustained trends and bleed in choppy, mean-reverting markets — so the conclusion may not generalise to other periods.

6.3Future work

  • Walk-forward validation. Re-tune parameters on a rolling in-sample window and trade them out-of-sample, so the sensitivity analysis becomes a genuine strategy rather than a hindsight exercise.
  • Broader universe & survivorship correction. Test across the full FTSE 100 (including delisted constituents) to remove sample bias.
  • Risk overlays. Add volatility-scaled position sizing, stop-losses, or a short leg, and compare against a risk-parity or volatility-target benchmark rather than naive buy-and-hold.
  • Statistical significance. Bootstrap the return series to attach confidence intervals to the Sharpe differences, distinguishing a real edge from noise.
